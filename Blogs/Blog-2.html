A Close Analysis of the Itch.io Homepage

This week I am looking at a website I commonly use and critically analysing all of its design elements, from its UI, its layout and flow, to its overall user experience(UX). For this I decided to look at Itch.io, which is a website I commonly use and check up on, and which I think is also very interesting to analyze because of how it works. Itch.io is primarily a storefront, or a catalogue, of independently made(indie) games, where users can freely host games for free, and are allowed to freely put a price tag on their work. I say primarily for games because it also has other stuff it hosts, such as tools for game and digital development, resources for board games, and an entire social featureset where game competitions and events can be hosted.

The homepage will be the focus of this blog, as this is the portal that is supposed to bring users to all other locations on the site. Starting with an overall glance the site can be divided into a topbar, a sidebar, and a grid which takes up the majority of the space. This grid itself is subdivided into labelled sections, such as ‘Latest Featured Games’ or genres or user specific collections of titles recommended based on previous browsing. At the top of the grid is a featured game of the day which takes up an entire row, thus taking up the largest amount of screen space and attention of the user. This grid can be scrolled down for a bit, going beyond just being one full screen's worth of content. The topbar contains links to stuff for the user's account, as well as links to broad sections of the website like ‘community’ and ‘feed’. The sidebar is more specifically focused on browsing the sites catalogue showing various options for sorting out a search, such as by genre, by tag or by price. It also has links at the bottom to the website's official blog posts.

Looking at this page as a total unit, it is clear that it draws the users focus directly to the catalogue, and that this the primary purpose for which most of the buttons and options are designed for. It takes up the most screen space, with a visually driven UI that displays photos for every game and tool, with a mouse over producing a popup that shows more media from the specific game, as well as animating the display photo into a gif or movie. Hassenzahl and Tractinsky speak about how UX is more than just functional layout but also has an aesthetic element to guide, reward and interest the user in specific elements. Itch.io does this through it’s media driven displaying of games, giving both its featured, and all of its catalogue, a space to show a visual representation of the games, with user interest (in the form of a mouse over) getting rewarded with a more detail view of the visual and more visuals popping up. The games could be simply displayed as text with a small photo or logo, but as a visual medium they are well served with highly visual spots in the catalogue.

To quickly speak on the point of representation, as raised by Kidd in their book on representation, this is something that is actually fairly important to think about with a storefront like itch.io that seeks to represent alternative and independent games (Kidd, 2015). The featured games of the day are curated by the team behind itch.io, they select a game based on personal knowledge, access to the author or various other somewhat opaque roads. This means there is power and importance behind the games itch.io decides are worth featuring, and what it says about their storefront as a whole, who is welcome there and who is not. So this simple list of games that pops up everyday could be a tool for positive representation or a way of gatekeeping out games the team might think don’t fit the brand of itch.io, for better or worse. Understanding the role of this feature is very useful for future analysis of how well the site does this.

Kidd, J. (2015). Theories of Representation in Representation. Oxfordshire: Routledge, pp. 18-39.
