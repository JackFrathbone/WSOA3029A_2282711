A Close Reading of “Overview of Data Visualization” by Qi Li

Data, the storage of information for various purposes, has always had an important place in human society for organisation and understanding. In this chapter of Embodying Data by Qi Li, the author discusses the history and uses of data visualization as a tool for understanding data. Breaking down data visualization into a few categories, the first one brought up is traditional data visualization which focuses on “communication for understanding” as its primary purpose (Li, 2020: 17). Visualizing data can allow for large amounts of data to be made easily understandable, as well as making clear trends in the data which can be difficult to understand without visual reference. The ability of traditional data visualization is to make data and its trends, or emergent qualities, easily accessible to wider audiences, or at least make vast incomprehensible data more understandable for human analysis.

While data visualization has existed for a long time, and has had many analogue forms, the computer age has allowed for a wide range of computer generated graphs, plots and infographics which easily allow data to be visualised and follow shared standards. In both sharing data with the public and for research this has become a common and very useful tool for sharing data. Because of its various forms, there are some differing opinions on what exactly defines data visualization, from simply being specifically computer generated images, to more broadly just being any visual representation of data that aids in understanding of said data. Li also personally argues that a central part of data visualization is the transformation of data into information, saying that while data is organisation of raw information without meaning, it becomes information through visualization, that is gaining meaning and becoming understandable. This creates a dichotomy of unprocessed information, or data, and process information, with the visualization of some form being a key point of transformation that is required to make data meaningful.

Li goes on in the chapter to list a variety of specific forms of data visualisations, such as graphs, plots, volumes and maps. These all highlight how various datasets can be turned into visual forms that take advantage of the specific type of information to be processed, and the best way to make it readable. After showing these examples, the author then goes on to list what they believe are the main characteristics of traditional data visualization: readability, recognizability and meaning (Li, 2020: 41). This leads on to the next section which emphasizes the importance of human perception in understanding data that has been visualised, raising some psychological examples of theories of perception and attention, relating them to data visualisation.

Over Li raises many interesting points and perspectives on what can seem like a very obvious concept, of course data needs to be visualised in some way to make it understandable for people, but the actual reasons why and how it should be done play an important role. Li shows how useful a tool data visuation is, and thus how necessary it is to correctly understand its powers and limitations, how it functions and the effect of visualization on the understanding of information. Overall it shows how critical thinking must always be applied, even to the seemingly mundane ways we understand the world through data.
